.✦ ݁˖ Little Women: an Unstructured Rant

"..I gave up everything you didn't like. I'm happy I did it. It's fine, and I waited, and I never complained because I.. I figured you'd love me, Jo."
- Theodore "Laurie" Lawrence, Little Women

My mother bought me the book "Little Women" by writer Louisa May Alcott when I was in high school. From then on, I've always regarded it as one of my favorite books, along with "Tentang Kamu" and "Hujan" by writer Tere Liye. Little Women resonated with me because it tells the everyday, domestic story of four sisters. Love in all it's forms: familial, romantic, platonic love.

To summarize, Little Women tells a story of the poor March family consisting of 4 sisters, a mother, and a father. The 4 sisters, in order of age, are Margaret "Meg" March, Josephine "Jo" March, Elizabeth "Beth" March, and Amy Curtis March. They are neighbors with the wealthy Lawrence household, consisting of Theodore "Laurie" Lawrence, his grandfather, and an honorary member: John, Laurie's tutor, who would end up married to the eldest March daughter.

There are many, many, many things I love about this book, as well as Greta Gerwig's movie adaptation of it in 2019. It is quite a realistic, wholesome story that could plausibly happen to anyone, especially at that time period. It makes it easier for me to resonate and relate to the story. Characters aren't inherently "good" or "bad", some of their actions make them complicated and deep, just as humans are in real life. For example, youngest daughter Amy March would burn Jo March's novel drafts because she felt left behind by her. Jo, still feeling angry about this, would go on to ice skate with neighbor Laurie, Amy wanting so desperately to come with them despite not being asked to join. Amy ends up falling into a hole of ice cold water, leaving Jo feeling guilty about being angry in the first place, even if her anger was not misplaced. Does this make Amy a bad character? An antagonist? No, it makes her a little sister who wanted to be involved, to be included, and when she wasn't, she immaturely retaliated. Does Jo getting mad at Amy make her the antagonist? No, it makes her a rightfully angry sister. I see many arguments online about the sisters' characters. Is Amy wicked? Is Meg selfish? I think they are simply their own characters.

On the topic of Amy March, she's actually my favorite sister of the four. I feel like I understand her very much. Her scenes in the movies were the most interesting to me, how she thinks, how she carries herself, the way she was ready to do whatever was needed to do in order to live comfortably despite having to make sacrifices. One of my favorite quotes by her:

"And as a woman, I have no way to make money... So don't sit there and tell me that marriage isn't an economic proposition because it is. It might not be for you, but it most certainly is for me." Amy Curtis March to Theodore Lawrence.

This quote to me solidified her as my favorite sister. She's always been regarded as the youngest, most naive. Louisa May Alcott wrote that among the sisters, she's perhaps the most beautiful with her golden blonde hair, her blue dress, her affinity for fashion. But this quote shows just how aware, how intelligent she is.

To me, Amy reads as one of the more intelligent sisters. People generally agree that Jo is perhaps the smartest of the bunch, but to me, Amy might take that cake. She's fully conscious of everything going on around her. While Jo has been rebellious to an extent since she was young, Amy has always been the observer, the golden haired youngest daughter who yearns to become rich. What I'm trying to say is that people like to think that Jo had it bad. She had it hard. I don't disagree. Jo faced her own wars. However, that doesn't make Amy's own wars any easier. Jo has always had that freedom, while Amy did not. While it seemed like a blessing when Aunt March chose her to travel to Europe with, as the book progresses, it becomes clear that Amy wants the end goal: to marry well, and she's willing to do what it takes, like when she considered accepting a proposal from a man she holds no affection for. Is that really.. a blessing?

"Don't marry him (Fred Vaughn)... you know why." - Theodore Lawrence, to Amy Curtis March.

"No, Laurie, that's mean ... I have been second to Jo my whole life in everything and I will not be the person you settle for just because you cannot have her. I won't do it, not when - not when I've spent my entire life loving you." - Amy Curtis March, to Theodore Lawrence.

Getting into the romance part in Little Women. In general, readers and audience are torn between two: Laurie & Jo, or Laurie & Amy. For context, Laurie develops feelings for Jo first, and then was rejected by Jo because she only saw him as a brother, a close friend. He would later develop feelings for Amy. Now, this is a battlefield in the Little Women discussion. Some say that he'd take any March sister, it doesn't matter who. I don't think this is true. If this were true, then he would've made a move on the eldest, Meg March, earlier in the book when he ran into her at a social event, and used their chemistry there to build a romantic relationship, but he didn't. He genuinely developed feelings for Jo.

After he was rejected, I believe he threw himself into studying. Arts, if I'm not mistaken, and would cross paths with Amy while traveling Europe to pursue this. Amy does not immediately throw herself at Laurie despite later finding out she's loved him all her life, neither does Laurie who was still in the feels about Jo rejecting him. Amy even states she feels sorry for him when he points out his ring is from Jo.

"I feel sorry for you, I really do. I just wish you'd bear it better." - Amy Curtis March, to Theodore Lawrence.

But did you know, dear reader, I genuinely forgot if they showed this in the movies or not, but the ring would end up in a drawer of Laurie's, retired, shortly after he realizes he's been slowly but surely falling for Amy March. THIS is so symbolic to me. It's him, realizing he no longer has feelings for Jo, and retiring the ring he loyally wore even after he was rejected because it was from her. I will never understand anyone who says he didn't genuinely love Amy. It's clear as day that indeed, he had fallen for her. The way the main characters in his play began having golden hair and blue attires, (sound familiar?), and how he looks forward to Amy's letters. Laurie even addresses this with Jo after he was engaged to Amy, saying he genuinely does feel care for Jo, but the love he feels for Amy is different.

Anywho. That is about all I should say about this. I've been ranting for quite some time now and I'm afraid this page will become absurdly long if I keep going. There is simply so, so much to unpack in Little Women. Watch it if you haven't, read it if you haven't!
