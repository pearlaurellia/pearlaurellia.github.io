.✦ ݁˖ Animating In Blender

Every video was animated by myself!

When I first decided to learn how to 3D model, my main motivation was to show off to my parents. Seriously. I wanted to show my mom and dad, and say "Look what I can do!"

My first project was a cup. A wonky tea cup. Sadly, I no longer have access to the original render because I have switched laptops since then. I then moved on to fancy and ambitious teacups, the Blender learner rite of passage donut, all the way up to some failed, cursed-looking character that I'm actually grateful I lost the files to. It would induce nightmares for you, probably, if you were to see it.

This was my very first render in Blender.
A static render of a teacup. 		This was my second ever render in Blender.

At the time, I didn't have the right device to properly start animating. It was quite painful. See, in order to make an animation, Blender would have to render every single frame one. by. one. The pudding animation above has around 96 frames. One frame takes around 3-5 minutes or so, and that's already using only 5 iterations/samples for each frame. How many seconds did I get out of 96 frames? 4 seconds. My previous computer, bless it and its components, did not stand a chance.

With my new laptop, I've produced 3 animations including the pudding, all of them under 10 seconds, but all of them I am quite happy with.

The animation on the right is my first take on this style of animation. I've done other simpler ones like girls on swings - just simple back and forth, manipulating the XYZ axises in the rotation, keyframe for each end point. However, I did lost many of my previous renders.

The animation on the left is my latest take on this style of animation.
From studying both 3D modeling and animation, and video editing, I've learned that logic skills are very important when pursuing the hobby. Keyframes are pretty self-explanatory, so if you can come up with a logical way to arrange the keyframes to achieve the animation you want, you can basically do anything you want. Once you start knowing what each type of physics mean in Blender, you can make any object bend to your will. Once you know that the boolean modifier in Blender can make a window, then you'll also know it can make holes in any other object, and now you've unlocked a whole new realm of possibilities.

As long as you give it enough time and practice, you can go from 1 to 100 in quite a short amount of time.
